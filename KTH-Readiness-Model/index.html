The KTH Innovation Readiness Level model

A shared, reasonably objective language for describing how far a venture has moved from idea toward market. This page is a reusable reference for program managers who want to adopt the model in their own program context.

Reusable across programs
01 · Orientation

What this is, and how to use it

The KTH Innovation Readiness Level™ is a model for measuring how far a venture has moved from idea toward market, across six independent axes each scored 1 to 9. It is used throughout the Fast Track Blue Team as the shared structure for what "reduce uncertainty" actually means: it gives every founder and every coach the same map, so de-risking stops being a vague instruction and becomes movement on named axes.

This page lifts the model out of that specific handbook so any EPFL program manager can reuse it. Everything below is the model itself and the way we apply it, not a set of Blue-Team-only rules. The cadence, escalation ladder and founder requirements that surround it in the handbook are program-specific and deliberately left out here: the model is portable, the operating procedure around it is yours to design.

What it gives you

One shared map

Six named axes every founder and coach can point to, instead of a vague "de-risk it".

What it is not

Not a grade

A low score measures distance to market, not idea quality. Moving down for the right reason is progress.

How to adopt it

Structure only

Use it to place a venture and agree milestones. Keep your own cadence, coaching and decisions around it.

The line we give founders

Support exists to help you increase validation across these dimensions, so you reach a clear, less-risky roadmap to scale. Or to help you kill the project fast, before more time is spent on something the evidence will not support. Both are wins. The only failure is staying busy in the middle, accumulating activity without moving any dimension and without learning enough to stop.

02 · The shared structure

The six readiness dimensions

The model scores six dimensions independently, each from 1 to 9. A composite average hides the weakest axis behind the strongest, so the axes are always worked separately.

CRL · Customer

Is the need real, and will they adopt and pay?

TRL · Technology

Does the solution work, and is it fit for purpose?

BRL · Business model

Is there a viable and sustainable model around it?

IPRL · IPR

Is the intellectual property identified, owned, and protectable?

TMRL · Team

Are the right people present, with capacity and alignment to execute?

FRL · Funding

Is there enough runway to reach a viable model?

A low score is not a bad mark. The model measures how far a venture has moved from idea toward market, not how good the idea is. Ventures move down as often as up, because one hard piece of evidence can reveal a founder was further from market than they thought. Moving down for the right reason is progress: they now know something true that they did not know before.

03 · The scale

How the 1–9 scale works

Each of the six areas is scored independently on a nine-point scale, visualised as a thermometer. Every level comes with defined criteria, milestones and activities, and a venture has to meet all of a level's milestones before it can advance to the next. The scale runs from an early-stage idea or hypothesis through to an innovation proven on the market. As a general reading:

Levels 1–3Explore
Levels 4–6Develop & verify
Levels 7–9Ready for market

Indicative only. The full 1–9 detail for each axis is in Appendix B below.

04 · Putting it to work

Using it in your program

The model says what a venture must have achieved, never how to get there. The how is the founder's own de-risking work; your coaching is the multiplier on it. Four rules keep the model honest whatever program wraps around it.

  • Target the lagging dimension, not the average. A composite score hides the weakest axis behind the strongest. A founder strong on technology and weak on customer is not "average", they are at risk on customer, and that is where support goes. A gap of more than two or three steps between dimensions holds the whole venture back, so the gap is the target.
  • Measure evidence at the current level, not speed of climbing. Deep tech, medtech and anything regulatory move up the scale slowly and legitimately. A founder meets the bar by producing real evidence at their current level, even when the number is not moving fast. The trigger for concern is always "no de-risking work", never "too slow for their field".
  • Moving down can be progress. Hard evidence sometimes reveals a venture was further from market than it thought. Knowing that is a gain, not a setback; help the founder act on it instead of defending the original plan.
  • The model says what, not how. The levels define what a founder needs to have achieved. They never prescribe how. Turn a level number into next week's work with two questions: what evidence would the next level require, and what single experiment produces it?
The coaching move

For the lagging axis, ask two questions: what evidence would the next level require, and what single experiment produces it? That converts a level number straight into a concrete next step (assumption → test → evidence → decision), whatever session format your program uses.

Appendix A · Reference detail

The model in detail

The model builds on NASA's original Technology Readiness Level (TRL) and extends it with five further areas — customer, business model, IPR, team and funding — that turn a working technology into a viable company. Each of the six areas is assessed independently on its own 1–9 scale, from an early-stage idea through to an innovation proven on the market.

Use it for structure only: a shared, reasonably objective language that lets founders and coaches describe where a venture stands without it becoming a matter of opinion. The model is deliberately generic, aimed at early-stage and technology-based ideas, and aligns with the lean startup method and the business model / lean canvas that founders already use.

The six readiness levels

One-line definition of each area.

AreaWhat it confirms
CRL · CustomerConfirm customer need and interest.
TRL · TechnologyDevelop and test the technology, product, service, or concept.
BRL · Business modelEstablish that the concept can be financially, environmentally, and socially viable and feasible.
IPRL · IPRClarify the legal and IP situation and secure relevant IP protection.
TMRL · TeamSecure the right competencies and align the team.
FRL · FundingSecure the necessary funding to take the idea to the market.

Appendix C · Practitioner notes

Moving between levels & reading a profile

The model names what a venture must have achieved, never how to get there. But each scale has an internal logic that tells you what kind of work raises that axis. Use it to turn a level number into a concrete next step.

The spine of each scale

AreaWhat raises this axis
Customer · CRLLevels 1-4 are about understanding the need (discovery, problem-solution fit); 5-9 about getting the solution into users' hands and selling (sales process, agreements, traction).
Technology · TRLClimbs with environment realism, integration and prototype finish: test a more complete prototype in progressively more realistic settings.
Business model · BRL1-3 describe the model in increasing detail; 4-6 calculate viability from assumptions and feedback; 7-9 prove it with pilots and real sales.
IPR · IPRL1-4 identify, describe and assess what is protectable; 5-9 act on a strategy: file, enter national phases, secure and manage rights.
Team · TMRL1-4 the team to validate the idea; 5-6 assemble the founding team; 7-9 build and scale the organisation. Levers: competence, capacity, alignment, diversity.
Funding · FRL1-4 fund the verification that finds a viable model; 5-9 fund realising it. External funding becomes the focus from level 5.
The coaching move

For the lagging axis, ask two questions: what evidence would the next level require, and what single experiment produces it? That converts a level number straight into next week's prep template (assumption → test → evidence → decision).

Reading a profile

  • A score is a per-axis snapshot of distance to market, not a grade. Low is not bad, and axes move down as often as up when real evidence arrives.
  • Read the shape, not an average. The lowest axis is the binding constraint, and that is where support goes.
  • Mind the gap. More than two or three steps between axes becomes inhibiting and raises failure risk. Close the gap before pushing the leaders higher.
  • Calibrate per venture by first defining what level 9 means for this specific case (deep tech and software differ), then reassess every three to six months.

The axes are also interdependent, in this order: customer and business model shape the technology and vice versa; those three shape IPR; all of them shape the team; and all together shape funding. A lagging upstream axis caps the value of downstream progress.

Customer ⇄ Technology ⇄ Business model→ IPR→ Team→ Funding

Two profiles, same scores read differently

BalancedAxes within a step or two of each other. Push the lowest. Lagging customerHigh TRL, low CRL: built before the customer was validated.

The red profile is the classic engineering-led shape: strong technology, unvalidated customers. Funding and team cannot compensate for the gap, so support goes to customer discovery and structured experiments, not more building. This is the readiness-level version of motion versus progress.
