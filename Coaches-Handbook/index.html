Blue Team Mentor Handbook

The shared method for everyone mentoring founders on the Blue Team. It explains what the support is for, how a session runs, and how we keep three different mentors applying the same logic the same way.

For external Blue Team mentors Reference, not founder-facing
01 · Welcome

What this handbook is for

You are part of the Blue Team: the mentors who work alongside founders to help them reduce the uncertainty in their venture. This handbook is your reference for how that support works at EPFL, what we ask of founders in return, and how to run sessions so they produce real movement rather than pleasant conversation.

Its single purpose is consistency. When several mentors each improvise their own approach, founders get mixed signals and the hard conversations land differently depending on who they happen to be talking to. This handbook gives everyone the same map, the same cadence, and the same language, so support feels coherent across the whole team.

Method

The readiness-level model gives every founder and mentor the same axes to work on.

Cadence

Observable actions

A weekly prep, a monthly update, a mid-term review. Things a founder visibly does.

Fairness

A separate, softer version of this material is shared directly with founders. This one is for you: it states the reasoning behind the rules so you can apply them with judgement.

02 · The bigger picture

Why we invest in founders

EPFL supports founders as a strategic investment, not a public service and not charity. Turning research and talent into companies is part of how Switzerland keeps its economy strong: more jobs created here, more capability retained in the country, more value generated locally. A finite, public resource is placed behind a founder in the expectation of a return: a venture that becomes strong enough to stand on its own.

There is a specific reason institutions, rather than the market, carry this stage. External support almost always requires maturity before it engages. Investors, corporate partners, banks and competitive funding bodies price risk, and at the earliest stage the uncertainty is too high for them to commit. That leaves a gap at the front of every venture's life, where the idea is most uncertain and the outside world is least willing to back it. Institutional support exists to cover exactly that gap.

The role, precisely

We help a founder de-risk their project to the point where they can connect with the next step: the first external milestone, the first investor conversation worth having, the first pilot customer, the first competitive grant from a body outside EPFL. We are a bridge to that point, not a destination. The aim of the support is to make the founder ready to no longer need it.

This framing matters for your work because it sets the direction of travel. A founder who stays comfortable on support without reducing uncertainty is moving away from the goal, not toward it. Recognising that early, and naming it kindly, is part of helping.

03 · Positioning

Your role as a mentor

Support is a finite, allocated resource, invested where founders convert it into reduced uncertainty. Your mentoring is a multiplier on the founder's own de-risking work, not a substitute for it. A multiplier applied to zero returns zero, which is the whole reason the cadence in Section 5 exists.

The founder's job between sessions is not only to build. It is to produce evidence that kills or confirms the assumptions the venture depends on. Building without testing produces motion, not progress, and motion is the thing founders most often mistake for traction. A large part of your value is keeping the founder honest about that difference.

Two framings that work, and one to avoid

Use

Scarcity

Our time is finite and we choose where it goes. Be transparent about how we choose.

Use

Identity

This is what founders who make it actually do between sessions. Hold that standard.

Avoid

Debt

Never tell a founder they "owe" us effort. Obligation framing produces performed effort, not real testing.

Where you sit relative to EPFL staff

You mentor the founder and apply the cadence. You flag patterns when the exchange starts to break down (missed prep, no evidence, no de-risking). The EPFL program team owns the continuation decisions and runs any formal step-back conversation. You are never on your own for the hard part, and you are not the one who ends support. See Section 7.

One rule underpins everything we require: it must be something a founder visibly does or does not do. We never require a state of mind. "Understand the value of support" is unenforceable and we do not use it. "Submit a prep template before booking time" is observable, fair, and survives challenge.

04 · The shared structure

The readiness-level model

We use the KTH Innovation Readiness Level model as the shared structure for what "reduce uncertainty" actually means. We use it for structure only: it gives every founder and every mentor the same map, so de-risking stops being a vague instruction and becomes movement on named axes. Each dimension is scored 1 to 9.

CRL · Customer

Is the need real, and will they adopt and pay?

TRL · Technology

Does the solution work, and is it fit for purpose?

BRL · Business model

Is there a viable and sustainable model around it?

IPRL · IPR

Is the intellectual property identified, owned, and protectable?

TMRL · Team

Are the right people present, with capacity and alignment to execute?

FRL · Funding

Is there enough runway to reach a viable model?

A low score is not a bad mark. The model measures how far a venture has moved from idea toward market, not how good the idea is. Ventures move down as often as up, because one hard piece of evidence can reveal a founder was further from market than they thought. Moving down for the right reason is progress: they now know something true that they did not know before.

The line we give founders

Support exists to help you increase validation across these dimensions, so you reach a clear, less-risky roadmap to scale. Or to help you kill the project fast, before more time is spent on something the evidence will not support. Both are wins. The only failure is staying busy in the middle, accumulating activity without moving any dimension and without learning enough to stop.

Three working rules

  • Target the lagging dimension, not the average. A composite score hides the weakest axis behind the strongest. A founder strong on technology and weak on customer is not "average", they are at risk on customer, and that is where support goes. A gap of more than two or three steps between dimensions holds the whole venture back, so the gap is the target.
  • Measure evidence at the current level, not speed of climbing. Deep tech, medtech and anything regulatory move up the scale slowly and legitimately. A founder meets the bar by producing real evidence at their current level, even when the number is not moving fast. The trigger for concern is always "no de-risking work", never "too slow for their field".
  • The model says what, not how. The levels define what a founder needs to have achieved. They never prescribe how. The how is the founder's own de-risking work between sessions, and your mentoring is the multiplier on it.
05 · The exchange

Cadence & requirements

We make the exchange explicit so that any later step-back reads as a known rule taking effect, not a personal decision by a mentor.

We commit

Regular coaching access, network and partner introductions, grant access, and the right to present as a current member of the program while active.

We require

The observable actions below, on this cadence. Set once by the program, applied to everyone the same way.

CadenceWhat the founder submits
Weekly
supports coaching		 A prep template, submitted at least 24h before each coaching session. Five fields (see below). No prep, no booked time — the missed-template rule is set by the program and applied without exception.
Monthly
tracks evolution		 An update against the founder's own intake targets: progress on key metrics or milestones, what moved, what did not, what changed in the plan. The reference point is their own trajectory, never an external benchmark.
Mid-term
progress review		 A structured review against the trajectory agreed at intake. The formal checkpoint where continued investment is explicitly reconfirmed or flagged. Output is recorded.
End
final report		 Outcomes, learnings, and what the support produced. Closes the loop and feeds future cohort decisions.

The weekly prep template

Before each session the founder names, in five lines:

  1. The assumption being tested this week.
  2. What was done to test it.
  3. What was learned — the evidence, not the activity.
  4. The decision that learning informs.
  5. The specific ask for this session.
Watch for going through the motions

A template that is consistently full of activity but empty of evidence (things done, nothing learned, no decision) is a partial miss, and it is meant to be caught. A founder who fills the form without doing the work is the exact failure the form exists to surface. Field 3 is the one that matters most.

06 · In practice

Running a session

The prep template is the session's spine. If it arrives, you already know the assumption, the evidence, the decision and the ask before you start, and the hour goes to the decision rather than to a status recap.

  • Start from the template. If none was submitted, apply the program's missed-template rule rather than holding the session as normal. The first exception turns the requirement into a suggestion.
  • Push for evidence, not activity. "I built the landing page" is activity. "Twelve of forty target users clicked through, none paid" is evidence. Keep returning to field 3.
  • Anchor on one dimension and one assumption. Name which readiness axis this week's work is de-risking. Resist spreading the session across all six.
  • Separate motion from progress, out loud. Busy weeks with no tested assumption are the warning sign, not the reassurance.
  • Reframe disconfirming evidence as a win. If the evidence moved the founder down a level for the right reason, that is progress. Help them act on it instead of defending the original plan.
  • Close on the decision and the next ask. Every session should end with what the founder now decides and what they will test next.
Your job in one line

Multiply the founder's de-risking work. Hold the standard that real founders test their assumptions between sessions, and make every hour move a named dimension.

07 · When the exchange breaks down

Recognising disengagement

When a founder stops doing the de-risking work, support is no longer multiplying anything, and continuing it does not help them: it keeps them comfortable while the venture quietly stalls, and our involvement makes that decline look endorsed. The program responds with a defined ladder that protects two things at once: our resources (we stop spending them where they are not used) and our reputation (a founder who is stepped back from should never be able to say truthfully that it came from nowhere).

Your part

You observe and flag. When the patterns below appear, record them and raise them with the EPFL program team. The program runs the steps and owns the decision to reduce or end support. You are never asked to deliver a termination on your own.

The patterns to flag

  • Two consecutive missed weekly prep templates.
  • A missed monthly update.
  • Three consecutive prep templates submitted but empty of evidence.
  • Building continuing, but no assumption being tested and no dimension moving.

The trigger is always "no de-risking work", never "too slow for their field". A founder producing real evidence at their current level is meeting the bar, even if the number is barely moving.

What your flag sets in motion

For context, so you understand where a flag leads. Each step is triggered by an observable threshold, is recorded, and leaves a reversal path open until the final one.

Step 0 · Active

Requirements met

Cadence in Section 5 is being met. Full support continues.

Step 1 · Confront

A direct conversation, with a reset

Trigger: 2 consecutive missed weekly preps · or 1 missed monthly update · or 3 consecutive empty templates.

The program names the specific misses, restates the allocation rule, and sets a concrete reset: what needs to happen, by when. The conversation and the reset date are logged.

Step 2 · Named disengagement

Involvement reduced, in writing

Trigger: no change by the Step 1 reset date.

The founder is told explicitly what reduces, why (the logged misses), and the specific, observable condition that reverses it. The path back is always stated, because this is a lever to restart the work, not a punishment.

Step 3 · Stop

Support ends

Trigger: the Step 2 reversal condition is not met by its deadline.

Support ends and active status is withdrawn, administered by EPFL. A founder always keeps the right to state truthfully that they were previously part of the program; we never erase a true past fact for ordinary non-performance.

Why this is fair

At every step the same answer holds: it was observable, it was recorded, the founder was told directly, and a way back was open until they declined it. Because the triggers are fixed thresholds rather than judgement calls, the decision tracks the founder's behaviour and not a mentor's patience or rapport.

Appendix A · Source & detail

About the KTH model

The readiness-level model used throughout this handbook is the KTH Innovation Readiness Level™, developed by KTH Innovation, the innovation office of the KTH Royal Institute of Technology in Stockholm. It builds on NASA's original Technology Readiness Level (TRL) and extends it with five further areas that turn a working technology into a viable company. It has been openly available under a Creative Commons (BY-NC-SA 4.0) licence since 2021.

We use it for structure only: a shared, reasonably objective language that lets founders and mentors describe where a venture stands without it becoming a matter of opinion. The model is deliberately generic, aimed at early-stage and technology-based ideas, and aligns with the lean startup method and the business model / lean canvas that founders already use.

The six readiness levels

KTH's own one-line definition of each area.

AreaWhat it confirms
CRL · CustomerConfirm customer need and interest.
TRL · TechnologyDevelop and test the technology, product, service, or concept.
BRL · Business modelEstablish that the concept can be financially, environmentally, and socially viable and feasible.
IPRL · IPRClarify the legal and IP situation and secure relevant IP protection.
TMRL · TeamSecure the right competencies and align the team.
FRL · FundingSecure the necessary funding to take the idea to the market.

How the 1–9 scale works

Each of the six areas is scored independently on a nine-point scale, visualised as a thermometer. Every level comes with defined criteria, milestones and activities, and a venture has to meet all of a level's milestones before it can advance to the next. The scale runs from an early-stage idea or hypothesis through to an innovation proven on the market. As a general reading:

Levels 1–3Explore
Levels 4–6Develop & verify
Levels 7–9Ready for market

Indicative only. The exact criteria for each of the 54 levels live in KTH's resource library; this handbook uses the model's structure, not a reproduction of its level text.

How we apply it on the Blue Team

  • Score each area separately. A single composite number hides the weakest axis; we work the dimensions individually.
  • Target the lagging dimension. A gap of more than two or three steps between areas holds the whole venture back, so the gap is where support goes.
  • Measure evidence at the current level. Slow-moving fields (deep tech, medtech, regulated) still meet the bar by producing real evidence; the concern is no de-risking, never pace.
  • Moving down can be progress. Hard evidence sometimes reveals a venture was further from market than it thought. Knowing that is a gain, not a setback.

Appendix C · Practitioner notes

Moving between levels & reading a profile

The model names what a venture must have achieved, never how to get there. But each scale has an internal logic that tells you what kind of work raises that axis. Use it to turn a level number into a concrete next step.

The spine of each scale

AreaWhat raises this axis
Customer · CRLLevels 1-4 are about understanding the need (discovery, problem-solution fit); 5-9 about getting the solution into users' hands and selling (sales process, agreements, traction).
Technology · TRLClimbs with environment realism, integration and prototype finish: test a more complete prototype in progressively more realistic settings.
Business model · BRL1-3 describe the model in increasing detail; 4-6 calculate viability from assumptions and feedback; 7-9 prove it with pilots and real sales.
IPR · IPRL1-4 identify, describe and assess what is protectable; 5-9 act on a strategy: file, enter national phases, secure and manage rights.
Team · TMRL1-4 the team to validate the idea; 5-6 assemble the founding team; 7-9 build and scale the organisation. Levers: competence, capacity, alignment, diversity.
Funding · FRL1-4 fund the verification that finds a viable model; 5-9 fund realising it. External funding becomes the focus from level 5.
The coaching move

For the lagging axis, ask two questions: what evidence would the next level require, and what single experiment produces it? That converts a level number straight into next week's prep template (assumption → test → evidence → decision).

Reading a profile

  • A score is a per-axis snapshot of distance to market, not a grade. Low is not bad, and axes move down as often as up when real evidence arrives.
  • Read the shape, not an average. The lowest axis is the binding constraint, and that is where support goes.
  • Mind the gap. More than two or three steps between axes becomes inhibiting and raises failure risk. Close the gap before pushing the leaders higher.
  • Calibrate per venture by first defining what level 9 means for this specific case (deep tech and software differ), then reassess every three to six months.

The axes are also interdependent, in this order: customer and business model shape the technology and vice versa; those three shape IPR; all of them shape the team; and all together shape funding. A lagging upstream axis caps the value of downstream progress.

Customer ⇄ Technology ⇄ Business model→ IPR→ Team→ Funding

Two profiles, same scores read differently

BalancedAxes within a step or two of each other. Push the lowest. Lagging customerHigh TRL, low CRL: built before the customer was validated.

The red profile is the classic engineering-led shape: strong technology, unvalidated customers. Funding and team cannot compensate for the gap, so support goes to customer discovery and structured experiments, not more building. This is the readiness-level version of motion versus progress.
